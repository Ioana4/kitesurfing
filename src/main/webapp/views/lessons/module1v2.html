Module 1 - Discovery

At this level you will learn and practice various skills on land that will transfer to the water for the next level. The skills covered include assessing the physical location and the wind window, using safety systems, managing proper equipment set-up, and piloting the trainer kite.

Anyone can complete this level, provided they meet the health requirements.

Location

Almost all accidents occur on land, so before going out, you have to chose a safe place to launch and land the kite. Keep in mind the wind conditions (straight, directions and stability) and the physical layout of the spot (potential obstacles, presence of secure area to operate the kite). Assess hazards in the area - both in and off the water. Identify the obstacles that may affect the quality of the wind. Never practice alone and do not venture further than you comfortably swim back.

Wind

It is important to know the strength and the direction of the wind to ensure you use the right kite size. The speed meter and local forecast will help you taking the right decision.

Wind Directions:

On Shore - Wind coming from the water to the land square to the beach

Off Shore - Wind coming from the land to the water square to the beach

Side Shore - Wind blowing from the sea towards the land, slightly side on or side off

Side On Shore - Wind blowing from the sea towards the land, slightly side on

Side Off Shore - Wind blowing from the land to the sea, slightly side off

Side Shore and side on shore are the safest wind directions for kiteboarding

Wind Effects

Obstacles can have a big effect on the wind, changing the flow enough to alter its direction or create turbulence. These effects can take place upwind, downwind or close to these obstacles.

Take great care never to let you kite fly into these areas.

Weather

Before you kiteboard get a local forecast for the day and have the right equipment for the condition. Learn about the weather signs that you can see around you.

The clouds are one of the best weather indicators around you. High-level clouds are cirrus clouds indicate that you are in a warm front or that one is approaching. This should mean stable weather and stable wind. If the clouds become fully established, then they will move into the midlevel and start to blanket out the sky. This could mean a grey and wet day if these clouds are dark and full of moisture.

The clouds forming at the low levels (cumulus) can be very messy and it means that you are in a cold front or that there is one approaching. The wind is mostly unstable and could lead to gusty wind conditions or sudden changes as heavy rain showers.

A cold front will start with low-level cumulus clouds that may then build up into the midlevel as the front establishes itself. This can lead to potentially stormy conditions, in certain cases to the formation of the cumulonimbus. This is characterized by its vertically developed shape and glaciated top that can extend to great heights of up to 12 km. As a cumulonimbus approaches, you will notice an increase in gusty winds and you may also feel the temperature drop. Usually once it has passed, the wind drops completely and the cloud disappears.

If the sky is black, chances are the weather is going to change and there is going to be a storm. In this case land you kite and wait until the clouds have gone. This type of cloud can also signal the start of an electrical storm. The kite lines make an excellent conductors for lightning! Pack the kite away!

Sea Breeze is a thermal wind, created by the sun heating the land, which in turn heats the air above. This warm air rises, creating a depression over the land. When this happens next to a body of cold water, the colder air from the sea moves in to fill the gap created by the rising warm air, which we feel as an on-shore wind. Sea breezes are generally very steady on-shore or cross-on-shore winds and can be great for kiteboarding. At the night, as the temperature drops, the whole process happens in reverse and the wind blows off shore (land breeze).

Right of Way

This is a long established sailing rule that applies to all forms of sailing, including kiteboarding. Respecting a few priority rules helps prevent accidents on the beach and on the water.

The rider entering the water from the beach has the right of way over the incoming rider.

Starboard rider has priority over oncoming rider and should keep the course and pass upwind with the kite higher.

If you passing upwind, bring the kite UP. If you are passing downwind, bring your kite DOWN.

When two riders are traveling in the same direction, the faster rider coming up from behind must give way to the slower rider in front.

The rider surfing a wave has priority over the one who is jumping or going in the opposite direction

Right of way must be given to other ocean and beach users. Kiteboarders must travel downwind to them.

To jump, a rider must have a clear safety zone of 50m downwind and 30 m upwind.

Trainer kite

In a IKO kiteboarding course, your instructor will begin your training by teaching you to fly a trainer kite and a small inflatable kite without power, on a short lines. You will learn how to master the basics first before taking on additional power with long lines. Your instructor will teach you to set up the equipment correctly, launch and land, use the safety systems, and perfect all of the basic flying skills you need to know at this stage.

Once you have learned the basics of flying a kite, you must to know how to stop the kite quickly in case of an emergency.

Set up and carrying the kite

The 2 lines located in the middle of the bar (front lines) must be connected to the leading edge bridle. These are the power lines. The other 2 lines located at the end of the bar (back lines) must be connected to the trailing edge bridle. These are the steering lines. Remember the phrase: Steer to the Rear .

There are two ways to set up your kite, with the bar and lines upwind and downwind, but all the time the colors have to match. Do not launch the kite with the bar inverted or crossed lines.

Handling the kite properly is essential to your safety and to those around you. You will learn how to set up an inflatable kite with 2, 4 or 5 lines and its safety systems.

Safety System

You must let go of the bar and pull the quick release in any dangerous situation including:

  • Any time you lose control of your kite
  • Whenever here is too much power
  • If you are being dragged
  • If you crash into another kite or object
  • If you are going to crash into something or someone

International Kiteboarding Signs

Your instructor will use these signs to guide you when voice cannot be heard. Learning these signs and using them with other riders will allow you to use a common language and help avoid misinterpretation.

Launch and land the kite with an assistant

To launch a kite safely you must first find the right angle to the wind.

Starting from a downwind position, walk the bar and lines upwind until you reach 90 degrees to the wind. You should walk upwind to put tension in the kite until the kite stops flapping. Give the thumbs p signal to the assistant and slowly bring the kite up to 45 degrees. Check the kite reaction: make sure it is no shaking, over powered or moving back and forth. If something feels wrong, land the kite to your assistant. If you lose control of the kite or become overpowered, let go of the bar and pull the quick release.

Just before you launch, look at your quick release to train your brain for a quick reaction in case of a problem.

Avoid positioning the kite at the top of the window, because an updraft could lift you. Never launch a kite directly upwind or downwind to obstacles (rocks, trees, buildings, houses etc)
